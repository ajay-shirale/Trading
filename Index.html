Module 1

Market Fundamentals

Before placing a single trade, you must understand the mechanics behind financial markets — who participates, why prices move, and how exchanges operate.



🏛️ What is the Stock Market?
▼
The stock market is a marketplace where buyers and sellers exchange shares of publicly listed companies. When you buy a stock, you're buying a fractional ownership in that business. Stock markets serve two primary functions: raising capital for companies (primary market via IPOs) and providing liquidity for investors to trade shares (secondary market). Major exchanges include NYSE, NASDAQ, LSE, NSE, and BSE.
📉 Bulls vs Bears & Market Cycles
▼
A bull market is a sustained period of rising prices (20%+ gain from recent low) driven by optimism, strong economics, and investor confidence. A bear market is the opposite — prices fall 20%+ from recent highs. Market cycles typically follow: Accumulation → Mark-up → Distribution → Mark-down. Understanding where we are in the cycle determines optimal strategy — buy during accumulation, be cautious during distribution.
⚙️ How Exchanges Work
▼
Exchanges match buy and sell orders through an order book — a real-time ledger of all pending orders. Market makers provide liquidity by continuously quoting bid/ask prices. When you place a market order, it fills at the best available price. A limit order only fills at your specified price or better. Understanding order types is fundamental to execution quality and slippage management.
⚖️ Supply, Demand & Price Discovery
▼
Price is simply the point where supply meets demand. When more people want to buy an asset than sell it (excess demand), price rises. When selling pressure exceeds buyers (excess supply), price falls. Price discovery is the continuous process of finding this equilibrium. News, earnings, macro data — all impact supply/demand balance instantly, which is why markets can move violently on releases.
💱 Bid/Ask Spread & Order Flow
▼
The bid price is what buyers will pay; the ask price is what sellers want. The difference is the spread — the market maker's profit. Tight spreads indicate high liquidity; wide spreads signal thin markets. Order flow analysis studies the sequence of trades to infer institutional intent. Large block orders often signal smart money positioning before major moves.
📋 Types of Orders
▼
Market Order: Executes immediately at current price. Fast but no price guarantee.

Limit Order: Executes only at your price or better. Gives price control, but may not fill.

Stop Order: Triggers when price hits a level — used for stop-loss protection.

Stop-Limit: Triggers at stop price, executes as limit. Safer but can miss in gaps.

Trailing Stop: Follows price automatically — locks in profit as trade moves in your favor.

Module 5 · Critical

Risk Management

The single most important discipline in trading. No strategy works without controlling how much you lose on each trade.

01
The 1–2% Rule
Never risk more than 1–2% of your total trading capital on a single trade. This ensures even a 10-loss streak doesn't devastate your account. At 2% risk, you'd need 50 consecutive losses to go broke — virtually impossible with any edge.
02
Always Use Stop Losses
Set your maximum loss before entering a trade, not during. Place stops at technically significant levels (below support, beyond structure), not at arbitrary round numbers. Moving your stop loss to accommodate a losing trade is the #1 account killer.
03
Maintain 1:2 Risk/Reward Minimum
Only take trades where your potential profit is at least 2x your potential loss. With a 1:2 R:R ratio, you can be wrong 60% of the time and still be profitable. Higher ratios (1:3, 1:4) give even more margin for error.
04
Position Sizing Formula
Shares to buy = (Account × Risk%) ÷ (Entry – Stop Loss). If your account is $10,000, risk 1% ($100), and your stop is $0.50 away, buy 200 shares. Never deviate from this formula based on "conviction" — your feelings are not data.
05
Diversification & Correlation
Don't concentrate risk in one sector or highly correlated assets. If you're long 5 tech stocks, you're essentially taking one big tech bet. True diversification means assets that don't move together — spread across sectors, asset classes, and timeframes.

Day traders open and close all positions within the same trading day — no overnight holds. This eliminates gap risk but requires intense focus, quick decision-making, and discipline to cut losses fast.

Module 9 · Often Overlooked

Trading Psychology

90% of traders fail — not because of bad strategy, but because of psychology. The market exploits every weakness in human nature.

😨
Fear of Missing Out (FOMO)
Chasing a stock after it has already moved significantly, entering at the worst possible time out of fear of missing the move.
💡 Rule: If you missed the entry, the next setup is coming. There are always more trades.
🤞
Loss Aversion & Hope Trading
Holding losers far too long hoping they'll recover, while cutting winners too early. Humans feel losses 2x more than equivalent gains.
💡 Rule: Let your stop loss do its job. Your opinion of the trade doesn't matter.
🎰
Revenge Trading
After a loss, immediately doubling down to "get even" with the market. This emotional decision-making often leads to catastrophic drawdowns.
💡 Rule: After 2 losses in a day, stop trading. The market will be open tomorrow.
💡
Overconfidence Bias
After a winning streak, traders believe they have a special ability and take outsized risks — then give back all profits on a single reckless trade.
💡 Rule: Process > outcome. Even good trades can lose; even bad setups can win.
⚓
Anchoring Bias
Fixating on a past purchase price ("I'll sell when I break even") rather than making decisions based on current market conditions.
💡 Rule: The market doesn't know your cost basis. Only current price action matters.
📖
Confirmation Bias
Seeking out information that confirms a pre-existing view while ignoring contradictory evidence. Leads to holding losers and ignoring warning signs.
💡 Rule: Actively seek evidence AGAINST your trade thesis before entering.

Trading Glossary

Master the language of the markets. Every professional term you need to know.

Ask Price
The lowest price a seller is willing to accept for an asset. When you buy at market, you pay the ask.
ATR (Average True Range)
A volatility indicator measuring the average range between high and low prices over N periods.
Bear Market
A market that has declined 20% or more from its recent peak, characterized by pessimism and selling pressure.
Bid Price
The highest price a buyer is willing to pay. When you sell at market, you receive the bid price.
Bull Market
A market rising 20%+ from a recent low, characterized by optimism, investor confidence, and buying pressure.
Candlestick
A chart representation showing open, high, low, and close prices for a given time period as a single unit.
Dead Cat Bounce
A temporary, short-lived recovery in a declining market before prices continue falling. False signal of reversal.
Delta (Options)
Measures how much an option's price changes for every $1 move in the underlying asset. Range: 0 to 1.
Drawdown
The percentage decline from a portfolio's peak value to its lowest point before recovering to new highs.
EMA (Exponential Moving Average)
A moving average that gives more weight to recent prices, making it more responsive to new information than SMA.
Fibonacci Retracement
Horizontal levels based on Fibonacci ratios (23.6%, 38.2%, 61.8%) used to identify potential support/resistance.
Float
The number of shares available for public trading. Low float stocks are prone to extreme price moves.
Gap Up / Gap Down
When a stock opens significantly higher or lower than the previous close, often on major news or earnings.
Golden Cross
A bullish signal when the 50-day MA crosses above the 200-day MA. Considered a major buy signal.
Implied Volatility (IV)
The market's forecast of likely price movement in an asset, derived from option prices. High IV = expensive options.
Liquidity
How easily an asset can be bought or sold without significantly affecting its price. High liquidity = tight spreads.
Margin
Borrowed capital from a broker to increase position size. Amplifies both gains and losses proportionally.
Market Capitalization
Total market value of all outstanding shares. Share price × total shares outstanding = market cap.
MACD
Moving Average Convergence Divergence — trend-following momentum indicator showing relationship between two EMAs.
Order Book
A real-time electronic list of all buy and sell orders for a security, organized by price level.
P/E Ratio
Price-to-Earnings ratio. Stock price divided by earnings per share. Measure of how expensive a stock is.
RSI
Relative Strength Index (0–100). Readings above 70 indicate overbought; below 30 indicate oversold conditions.
Scalping
A trading strategy capturing small price movements many times per day, with very short hold times.
Short Selling
Borrowing and selling shares hoping to repurchase them cheaper later. Profits when price falls.
Slippage
The difference between the expected execution price and the actual fill price, usually in fast-moving markets.
Support & Resistance
Price levels where buying (support) or selling (resistance) pressure is historically strong enough to reverse price.
Theta Decay
The rate at which an option loses value over time as it approaches expiration. Benefits option sellers.
VWAP
Volume Weighted Average Price — the ratio of value traded to total volume, used as intraday benchmark.
Volatility
The degree of price variation over time. High volatility means large swings; low volatility means stable prices.
Volume
Total number of shares/contracts traded in a period. High volume confirms price moves; low volume warns of fakeouts.
Wash Sale Rule
IRS rule: you cannot claim a loss if you rebuy the same security within 30 days before or after the sale.
Whipsaw
A condition where a security's price moves sharply in one direction then quickly reverses — trapping traders.
Yield Curve
A line plotting interest rates of bonds with equal credit quality but differing maturity dates. Inverted = recession signal.
